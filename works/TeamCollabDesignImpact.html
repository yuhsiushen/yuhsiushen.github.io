Improving team collaboration and tools is crucial. Initially, we used Sketch & InVision, which led to engineers frequently complaining about inaccuracies in Inspect.
In early 2021, I proposed transitioning our design drafts to Figma for development. I devised a migration plan and schedule to enhance collaboration efficiency and alleviate engineers' pain points when reviewing design drafts.

Approach

  1. Schedule Planning:
    Develop a timetable for migrating to Figma to ensure smooth progress.
  2. Tutorial Creation:
    Produce Figma usage tutorials to assist engineers in adapting to the new tool.

Outcomes

After using Figma for several collaboration cycles, engineers were surveyed for satisfaction, with most expressing approval of the improvements. Feedback on design annotation was also gathered to improve future collaboration efficiency.

üë©‚Äçüíª Designer X PMÔºöOptimized work flow

Product requirements change quickly, and when they do, PMs and designers are often the first affected. This issue is commonly brought up in retro meetings by PMs or designers.
To minimize the impact on designers and ensure smooth collaboration with PMs, I worked with the PM Lead to establish a workflow, ensuring close coordination between design and other departments.

Approach

  1. Timeline planning:
    Design processes are scheduled, and requirements are categorized into major and minor, with defined lead times for each.
  2. Regular reviewing:
    Prioritized requirements are estimated in sprints, and weekly reviews with the PM Lead ensure alignment and awareness of requirement changes.

Outcomes

After a few sprints, the problem wasn't mentioned in retro meetings anymore. We tracked team collaboration regularly and discussed ways to improve during retro meetings, making sure the collaboration between designers and PMs ran smoothly.

üïµÔ∏è Designer X RD & QAÔºöDesign QA work flow

With the growing complexity of our product, each feature or process release involves many details. Especially in interfaces with multiple layers of interaction, QA efforts are crucial. However, when QA lacks full understanding, it may lead to discrepancies in the final output.
To ensure the desired product experience, I introduced a Design QA process, fostering collaboration between designers and QA for quality control.

Approach

  1. Define QA roles:
    Clearly define the scope of Design QA to avoid overlap and confusion
  2. Establish priority and carding process:
    Prioritize and categorize issues, recording them on project management tool cards.
  3. Track issues by cards:
    Utilize project management tool cards to track the progress of bug fixes for efficient management.

Outcomes

At the beginning, there were about 20 UX UI issues per sprint, but after 6 months of process adjustments and collaboration with RD, the number of issues per sprint has dropped to below 5.

We used Google Docs and Sheets for document and project management, but it became challenging to search and track progress over time.
After assessing team needs, we found ClickUp, which met various department requirements, and successfully adopted it in 2022.

Process

Step1: Consolidate requirements

Consolidate everyone's requirements and find suitable tools to enhance the overall team's productivity.

Step2: Internal testing

Begin with internal testing within the design team and then promote it to other colleagues for reference.

Step3: Establish execution standards

Establish execution standards for ClickUp, ensuring that PMs, designers, RDs, and QAs can clearly see their respective tasks, enhancing the overall team's collaboration efficiency.
